# Chapter Two: Two Strikes

I was born December 8, 1972, on the lakefront at Pigeon Lake, Ontario. Most people are born in a hospital and brought home to a place. I was born already standing on the ground that would later try to kill me and end up making me instead.

## The First Strike

I was fifteen, maybe sixteen. I had my dog's leather collar in my left hand and a chain wrapped around a tree in my right. That's the last ordinary moment I remember having.

The bolt came down on the tree itself — split it clean from two inches above the grass all the way to the top, like the sky had taken an axe to it. Limbs fifteen feet long went flying into the lake. I went flying too. Thirty feet, and I landed on my back.

I didn't lose consciousness. That's the part people don't believe. My eyes were open the whole time and I could not move a single muscle in my body. And the only thing in my vision, covering everything, was amethyst — not purple like a flower or a bruise, but a purple brighter and more total than anything I've seen since, like the color itself had a voltage. Every organ in me felt like it detonated at once. I lay there for ninety seconds knowing I was alive but not sure my body had gotten the message yet.

When I finally got up, I walked into the house in my shorts. Just my shorts. Everything else I'd been wearing was gone — burned off or blown off, I still don't know which.

That's the night the 7th sense turned on. I started seeing things twice a day after that — flashes, like watching code scroll past faster than you can read it, except I could read it. Not vague feelings. Not "something's coming." Actual pictures of things that hadn't happened yet, sometimes twenty-five years out. I didn't ask for this. The lake didn't ask me either. It just rearranged something in me and left the rest up to what I'd do with it.

## The Second Strike

The second one came not long after I'd helped put out a kid who'd caught fire at Don Valley Junior High School in Toronto, on the football field. I want to be clear about the order, because it matters to me: the second strike didn't make me the kind of person who runs toward a fire instead of away from it. I already was that. The lightning just kept finding people like that.

The bolt hit the goalposts and didn't stop there — it tentacled, branched off in more than one direction, the way a crack spreads through glass. One arm of it caught a kid walking in front of me. Another arm caught me, walking behind. I felt the same thing in my chest that I'd felt at the lake — that same total completion — but smaller, like an echo instead of the original sound.

The kid wasn't as lucky. Third-degree burns over thirty percent of his body. Two others and I tore our coats off and beat the fire out of him on the ground until there was nothing left to put out. He lived.

I don't tell that story to make myself sound brave. I tell it because it's the second time the sky reached down and touched me, and the second time I walked away with something added instead of something taken.

## What It Gave Me

By the time I was twenty-seven, in 2000, I was telling my son's mother — while she was pregnant with him — that there was a war coming in Iraq. I didn't read it in a newspaper. I saw it the way I see everything now: images flashing through my head like code I already knew how to read, twenty-five years before it would matter to anyone else. I was right. I've been right more times than I can count since, and wrong rarely enough that I stopped doubting it a long time ago.

That's the foundation everything else in this book stands on. Before the courtroom, before the badge that put me in handcuffs, before I solved what happened to Dave — there were two strikes of lightning and a gift I never asked for and never gave back.
