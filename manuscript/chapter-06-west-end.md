# Chapter Six: West End

Before any of this — before the lake, before the badge, before the courtrooms — I was a kid in Mimico, on the west end of Toronto, who loved hockey more than almost anything.

My dad coached me starting when I was three years old. Most kids that age are still learning how to walk in a straight line, and I was already on skates with my father standing behind the bench, watching me. That's not a small thing. A lot of what I became — the stubbornness, the refusal to stay down, the willingness to get back up and skate at something again — started on that ice with him.

My dad worked at Harper's Detroit Diesel on the QEW. It was the kind of job that asks everything of a man's body and gives back a paycheck and not much else — the kind of work that built half the families in that part of Ontario. He'd put in his hours on that highway corridor and then show up at the rink to coach me anyway. I didn't understand what that cost him until I was much older. I just knew he was always there.

That's the part of my life people don't ask about, because it doesn't have lightning in it or a courtroom or a headline. But it's the part that built the foundation everything else stood on. I was an ordinary kid from a working-class family in Mimico, taught by his father to compete, to show up, to not quit on the ice even when you're losing. Everything that came later — the gift, the fights, the empire I'm building now — runs through that kid first.

My parents partied a lot back then — people over, drinks flowing, the kind of house where the adults forgot, sometimes, that there was a three or four-year-old paying attention to everything they left lying around. I found a lighter once and started a fire in the basement. Another morning I drank vodka someone had left out. Nobody was watching closely enough either time. I survived both, but I think about it now as a father myself, and I'll say plainly: parents need to be careful with what they leave within reach of a small child, especially now, with more dangerous things in more houses than there used to be.

Past that, I was never an inside kid. I lived outside — always one more critter brought home, always something with fur or scales that I'd decided needed me to take care of it. And by the time I was old enough to drive, I had some of the fastest cars in Mississauga. Looking back, that thread runs straight through everything: a kid who didn't sit still, who needed to be out in the world doing something, testing something, moving fast enough to feel it.
