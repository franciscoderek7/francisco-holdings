# Chapter Five: State-Created Danger

I'm not going to clean this part up to make myself look better. It wouldn't be honest, and it wouldn't be fair to anyone else living through the same thing right now.

Between the stress of the court cases, the breakdown between me and my kids' mother, and pain I'd been carrying for years without the legal medicine I was entitled to, I started using other drugs. Not because I wanted to escape my life, but because the pain didn't go away just because the system refused to let me treat it properly. When you take away a man's legal medicine and call it a paperwork issue, you don't make the pain disappear. You just make him find it somewhere else.

Then I met Gemma. She's the reason I got away from the harder drugs the first time. I want that on the record clearly, because it's the truth and because she's earned it: she pulled me back from something I might not have walked away from on my own.

Then COVID hit. We went to London, and somewhere in that stretch of isolation and disruption, we got pulled into fentanyl. I overdosed in 2020. Gemma saved my life that night. I am only able to write this book because of what she did in that moment.

I want to be precise about what happened in 2024, because it matters for more than just my own story. By then, Health Canada and the Attorney General still hadn't fixed my license — my postal code, my GPS location, the legal grow site that had been compromised back in 2013. I was still locked out of the legal system that was supposed to serve me. So I went back to the illicit market, because that was still the only place left to turn. I overdosed again in 2024.

There's a legal term for what happened to me, and once I learned it, I understood my own life better than I had before: state-created danger. It's the idea that a government doesn't only fail you by doing nothing — it can actively create the danger that hurts you when its own actions or refusals leave you with no safe path forward. Health Canada created the breach. The Attorney General refused to fix it for over a decade. Between those two facts sits a man with no legal way to get what he needed, pushed into a market that nearly killed him twice.

I'm not telling this story to make anyone feel sorry for me. I'm telling it because it's the clearest proof I have of what happens when a system breaks something and then spends years pretending it didn't. I lived it twice. I only survived it because Gemma was there the first time, and because something — call it the gift, call it stubbornness, call it the same thing that's kept me standing since I was a teenager on a lake — wasn't ready to let me go the second time either.

This is the chapter where the lightning, the system, and the law all collide. Everything before this was about what was done to me. This is about what I almost let it do to myself, and why I'm still here to write the rest of it.
