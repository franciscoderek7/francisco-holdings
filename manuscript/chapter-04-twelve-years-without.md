# Chapter Four: Twelve Years Without

My daughter Michaella is a genius. I don't say that the way parents say it about every kid — I say it because I've watched her think, and there's a clarity in how her mind moves that I recognize from somewhere I can't fully explain. She grew up and became a psychology graduate, which makes sense to me now looking back — she was always reading people before she could read books.

My son Dylan loves to build. Give him something in pieces and he'll find the order it's supposed to go in. While I was out doing what I did — running court cases across Canada, fighting for patients to get their licenses, living more on the road than at home — Dylan was home building things, quietly becoming the kind of person who creates instead of just consumes. I think about that a lot. I was out fighting a system. He was home learning how to put things together. Both of us were preparing for something, even if neither of us knew it at the time.

I wasn't home as much as I should have been during those years, and I won't pretend otherwise. I was on the road constantly — courtrooms in one province, then another, representing myself, representing the cause, getting patients their medical marijuana licenses one fight at a time. That work mattered. It still cost something at home, and I carry that.

Here's the part of the story that explains why I'm still in court today, decades later.

In 2013, Health Canada compromised my grow location. They mailed out envelopes as part of a mailing list error — and those envelopes exposed the grow locations of medical marijuana patients and growers across the country, mine included. It wasn't a hacker, it wasn't a leak from the black market — it was the government's own privacy breach. It took ten years for that to be found guilty in 2023, ten years for the system to admit what it did.

In the meantime, I lost the ability to use my own legal grow location. I have been without my cannabis — my actual licensed, legal cannabis — for over twelve years because of it. Once a location is compromised like that, you can't just move it and carry on. The system that's supposed to track and protect a patient's grow site instead refuses to update it. My postal code, my GPS location, sat wrong and dangerous for over a decade, and Health Canada and the Attorney General refused to fix it.

That left me exactly two choices: go to the illicit market to get what I was legally entitled to, or risk arrest trying to access what was rightfully mine through a system that wouldn't even acknowledge its own mistake.

That's why I'm suing the Attorney General. Not for sympathy, not for spite — because a government breach put me in an impossible position for over a decade and never once tried to fix it. Everything else in this book — the lightning, the stairs, Dave — happened to me. This is the one where the system did it to me on paper, in writing, with my own personal information, and is still pretending today that it didn't.
