# Chapter One: Pigeon Lake

I grew up between two worlds. North York gave me the city — concrete, noise, the kind of place that teaches you to move fast and watch your back. Pigeon Lake gave me the quiet, up north past Omemee, where the water sits still enough to show you your own face if you let it.

It was on that lake that I learned I had the gift.

I don't remember the sky cracking before it happened. I remember after. I remember feeling every organ in my body explode at once — not in pain the way you'd think, but in a kind of total rearrangement, like something reached inside me and reset every wire to a different frequency. I was lying there alive when I shouldn't have been. That's the only way I know how to say it. The lake didn't take me. It changed me instead.

People who haven't been struck by lightning think of it as an injury. I think of it as a download. Something got through that day that doesn't usually get through. I started seeing things differently after — not metaphors, not symbols. The actual code underneath things. The structure other people can't see because they were never forced to look.

I didn't have a name for it yet. I just knew the world looked different than it had the day before.

Dave Palmer was my friend for thirty years. Thirty years is long enough that a friendship stops being a relationship and becomes a fact about who you are — like a rib, like a fingerprint. Dave was that to me. He was shot five times with a .308 rifle. I'm not going to describe what that does to a body, because I don't need to make this dramatic — what it did to the people who loved him is enough.

What I will say is this: I didn't let it go. Whatever had rewired itself in me on that lake, it didn't switch off when it came to Dave. I saw what other people weren't seeing. I followed it. And the man responsible is in prison for life, no parole, because I didn't stop until that was true.

That's the part of my story people want to skip to — the lightning, the gift, the case that got solved. But none of it makes sense without the lake first, without North York first, without understanding that I was an ordinary person from an ordinary place before any of this happened to me. The gift didn't make me special. It got dropped on top of someone who was already stubborn enough to use it.

This book is the rest of that story.
